Many observers were shocked and saddened by the news of Mr. Khodorkovsky's arrest. ''It is a blow to the hopes of a better future for Russia,'' said Mikhail Kasyanov, a former prime minister at the time of Mr. Putin's election in 2000.

Some even suggested that Mr. Putin was beginning to look like Mr. Khodorkovsky's predecessor, President Boris N. Yeltsin, who was criticized for a haphazard style that often seemed to change course at the last moment.

''This is a very unexpected and surprising development,'' said Nikolai Petrov, head of the Carnegie Moscow Center's political analysis program. ''It's a total 180-degree turn from what this regime has been doing for the past four years.''

Mr. Petrov even raised the specter of the Soviet Union, noting that Mr. Khodorkovsky's arrest recalled the way the Kremlin handled Mr. Yeltsin's opponents in the Soviet days.

''This is a return to the Soviet past, to the 1980's and the practice of putting political opponents in jail,'' Mr. Petrov said. ''It's a logical next step for Putin, who is becoming more and more like Stalin.''
